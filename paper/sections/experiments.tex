\section{Empirical Findings}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Setup}

We evaluate 12 complete P1--P4 model runs on the VEH probes: model_C, \texttt{model_G}, model_H, model_E, model_A, model_D, model_B, \texttt{model_F}, \texttt{llama-3.3-70b}, model_K, model_L, and model_M. Here \(n=12\) denotes model-level observations with all four probes complete, not a development split. The headline metrics are P1-Composite, P2 final feasible power ratio, P3-Success, and P4 full-bank Kendall Tau. Calibration references, split-resolved tables, uncertainty intervals, full metric definitions, prompt templates, and the run-mode audit are in the appendix.

Table~\ref{tab:main_results} gives the core result. No model leads more than one headline stage: model_A leads P1, model_B leads P2, model_M leads P3, and model_C leads P4. The most revealing reversal is Gemini versus model_C: Gemini is the best feasible-set seeker in P2 but weak on P4 ranking, while model_C is the best policy-conditioned ranker but weak on P2 search. The 12-model view makes the same point more directly: model_L is near the P3/P4 frontier but weak on P1/P2, model_M moves only the P3 point-estimate frontier, and model_K remains below model_H on P2 and P4. Newer or more explicit thinking-capable models therefore shift specific boundaries rather than monotonically improving the whole workflow.

The within-stage details support the same interpretation without needing four long subsections. \textbf{P1 finding:} credible triage is not raw accuracy; model_A wins the composite through balanced action discipline, while models with acceptable raw accuracy collapse once missing-information and infeasibility discipline are scored. \textbf{P2 finding:} start is not finish; Gemini is not the best first-step model, but wins through sustained feasibility-preserving closure, and it is the only frontier model to beat the anchor-fixed calibration baseline. \textbf{P3 finding:} escape is not recovery; Gemini and Claude escape traps frequently but have very different cascade and final-success behavior. \textbf{P4 finding:} ranking is not one skill; full-order structure, exact endpoint agreement, and policy-active ranking separate. The full per-probe tables are in Appendix Tables~\ref{tab:appendix_p1_full}--\ref{tab:appendix_p4_full}.

We next quantify response-control profiles directly from logs. The extractor uses P1 action distributions and false-action rates; P2 edit deltas, feasibility preservation, directed repair, and objective traces; P2/P3 verifier-feedback reduction consistency; P3 escape, cascade, dead-budget, and recovery-quality signals; and P4 full-ranking, BARS, exact-match, policy-sensitive-pair, parse, and Pareto diagnostics. The complete 12-model profile table is in Appendix Table~\ref{tab:profile_quantification}. The intended diagonal relations are strong associations: action prior tracks P1-Composite (Spearman \(\rho=0.972\)), edit style tracks P2 ratio (\(\rho=0.839\)), and preference execution tracks P4 BARS (\(\rho=0.769\)) and full Tau (\(\rho=0.762\)). State trust is more structured: it does not monotonically track P3-Success because P3 splits into escape, cascade, and conversion to feasibility, but it is negatively correlated with P2 ratio (\(\rho=-0.39\)) and P4 BARS (\(\rho=-0.55\)). This is the key trade-off: a prior associated with strength at one boundary can be associated with weakness at another.

Table~\ref{tab:reasoning_vs_profile} rules out the shallow reading that DiagBench is only a reasoning-model leaderboard. Thinking-capable rows help the corrupted-state boundary on average, but they do not determine the P1, P2, or P4 frontier. We also separate capability from runtime configuration: model_A is a hybrid-thinking commercial model whose API default is documented as thinking-off, \texttt{model_G} was run with the provider-default DashScope call rather than an explicit \texttt{enable\_thinking=True} override, and the model_K/Mimo manifests record schema-stabilizing no-think controls. A more diagnostic account is boundary fit: P2 rewards bounded repair, P3 rewards state skepticism, and P4 rewards evaluator-role discipline.

\subsection{Mechanism Evidence}

P3 directly tests whether corrupted-state recovery is separable from clean-start repair. model_E reaches 62.5\% feasible coverage in P2 but only 16.0\% P3-Success; model_C shows the reverse shape, with weak P2 feasible coverage (36.1\%) but strong P3 recovery (42.3\%). This asymmetry motivates the state-summary intervention.

Table~\ref{tab:p3_intervention} shows that replacing raw trajectory continuity with verifier-authored \texttt{state\_summary} raises model-mean recovery success from 50.0\% to 63.2\% and lowers cascade from 28.1\% to 9.5\%. Claude gains the most in success (+33.3 points), consistent with its continuity-biased profile, while model_C and model_D show the clearest cascade reductions. Because the intervention changes prompt-facing state representation, we interpret it as evidence consistent with verifier-authored state isolation, not as a fully isolated causal proof against compression or formatting effects.

Figure~\ref{fig:mechanism_evidence} and Table~\ref{tab:isomorphic_probe_main} show a second mechanism: selection and generation are not interchangeable. Holding the underlying feasible design fixed, all four audited models perform best when selecting, worse when completing, and near floor when generating from a near-feasible seed. For model_C, Claude, and \texttt{model_G}, B-generation is parse-clean but wrong; for Gemini, the hardest generation form also induces syntax breakdown. This mirrors the P2-versus-P4 reversal: evaluative priors and generative search priors are distinct.

\paragraph{Controlled prompt intervention.}
The profile interpretation implies that behavioral deficits cause stage-specific underperformance, but Tier~1 correlation does not establish causation. We ran a pre-registered controlled-prompt experiment with four cells, each pairing a known deficit model with its weak stage. Every cell compares five prompt conditions---default, neutral (generic structured execution), targeted (boundary-specific diagnosis), wrong-boundary (mismatched diagnostic prompt), and a stacked variant for C2---under frozen seed-42 task sampling and 10,000 paired bootstrap resamples. All prompts are frozen; no iterative refinement occurred after pre-registration.

No cell shows a significant targeted gain (Table~\ref{tab:tier3_main}). C2 is the strongest signal but reverses: targeted success is 19.4~points \emph{below} neutral, while the structured neutral prompt lifts success 22.2~points above default (CI [0.083, 0.361]; Appendix~\ref{sec:tier3_full}). C3 shows borderline top-1 improvement (94\%$\to$100\%, two tasks) with negligible full-ranking gain. C4 masks a model interaction: targeted helps model_C modestly while Claude degrades and its parse-error rate rises from 5.0\% to 18.3\%; excluding parse errors, Claude's classification accuracy under targeted (0.694) exceeds neutral (0.617), isolating a format-compliance rather than reasoning failure.

The null pattern supports benchmark validity rather than weakening it. If the Tier~1 behavioral fingerprints were shallow prompt artifacts, targeted prompts should repair them. The absence of reliable targeted gains across four diverse model-stage pairs, together with C2's reversal, suggests that the fingerprints reflect structural prior-boundary compatibility, not superficial prompt sensitivity. A benchmark that disappears under prompt rewriting would not measure anything durable; that DiagBench resists prompt-level repair is evidence that its diagnostic signal is genuine.

\subsection{Cross-Domain Construct Validity}

We instantiate a hardened circuit-design audit to test whether the P1--P4 scaffold transfers beyond VEH. The updated audit replaces only P1 and P2 with 32 triage tasks and 32 repair tasks from circuit v3.1; P3 and P4 retain the original corrupted-state and policy-ranking banks. The task bank spans closed-form RC filters, loaded dividers, LED current limiters, op-amp amplifiers, and linear regulators. It is not a circuit benchmark; it is a construct-validity check with a different oracle, different constraints, and different trap geometry.

\subsection{Summary}

Taken together, the results support one claim: engineering-agent readiness is best diagnosed by prior-boundary compatibility. P1, P2, P3, and P4 are not four difficulty levels of one ability. They are four control boundaries that reward different response-control priors. No current frontier model matches all of them simultaneously. The controlled-prompt audit adds a further check: that these priors resist prompt-level repair reinforces their interpretation as structural boundary-compatibility signals rather than prompt-sensitivity artifacts.
